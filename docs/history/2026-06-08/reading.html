English
Scientists have discovered a key brain chemical that helps us break old habits and adapt to sudden changes in our environment. The research showed that when mice faced disappointment in a virtual maze—specifically when an expected reward did not materialize—there was a surge of the neurotransmitter acetylcholine, making them more likely to try new strategies. Conversely, when acetylcholine was blocked, the mice became less flexible and were more inclined to stick with outdated choices. This study not only enhances our understanding of the mechanisms behind behavioral flexibility but also has implications for treating conditions like addiction, obsessive-compulsive disorder, and Parkinson's disease, which make it difficult to break habits.
